Scenario A - Email Phishing
The Appointment
Rescheduling Email
You receive the following email on a Monday morning. You do have an upcoming hospital appointment, so the subject line feels familiar.
⚠ Phishing Email Impersonation Urgency Tactic Credential Harvesting
Simulated Email - Spot the Red Flags
Inbox
From: nhs-appointments@nhs-patient-portal.co ⚑ 1
Subject: URGENT - Your appointment requires immediate confirmation ⚑ 2
To: you | Mon 9:14 AM

Dear Patient,

Your upcoming appointment at Royal Hospital has been rescheduled. You must confirm within 2 hours or your slot will be released. ⚑ 3

Please log in to verify your details and confirm attendance:

Confirm Appointment Now ⚑ 4

NHS Patient Services | royalhospital.nhs-patient-portal.co ⚑ 5

⚠ Your email client flagged this message as possible spam.
⚑ Red Flag 1: Sender Domain
Real NHS emails come from @nhs.net only. "nhs-patient-portal.co" is a fake domain designed to look official.
⚑ Red Flag 2 : "URGENT" Subject
Phishing emails manufacture urgency to prevent you from thinking carefully. Legitimate NHS systems give adequate notice.
⚑ Red Flag 3 - 2: Hour Deadline
Artificial deadlines are a classic social engineering tactic. Real appointment changes never require confirmation within hours.
⚑ Red Flags 4 - 5 : Fake Links
Hover over NHS links before clicking. Real NHS links point to nhs.uk subdomains only, never third-party domains.

Scenario B - SMS Smishing
The Prescription
Renewal Text
You receive a text message on your phone. You do take regular medication, so a prescription reminder seems plausible.
⚠ Smishing (SMS Phishing) Credential Theft Payment Fraud
Simulated SMS: Spot the Red Flags
9:23 AM
Messages
NHS-RxRenew ⚑
Your NHS prescription is expiring. Renew now to avoid a £9.90 charge. Confirm details: nhs-rx-renew.com/confirm ⚑
⚑ Red Flag : Sender Name
"NHS-RxRenew" is not an official NHS sender ID. The NHS uses NHSApp or your GP practice name for legitimate SMS.
⚑ Red Flag: Fake Domain
"nhs-rx-renew.com" is not an NHS domain. All genuine NHS digital services use .nhs.uk addresses only.
⚑ Red Flag: Prescription Charge
NHS prescription charges (£9.90) are never collected via text message links. Charges are paid at your pharmacy.
✓ What a Real NHS Text Looks Like
Genuine NHS texts contain your name, come from your GP surgery, and never ask you to click a link to pay or confirm personal details.

Scenario C - Voice Phishing (Vishing)
The NHS
Data Quality Call
You receive a phone call from someone claiming to be from the Royal Hospital Patient Data Team. The caller sounds professional and knows your name.
⚠ Vishing (Voice Phishing) Social Engineering Identity Fraud
Simulated Call Transcript
CALLER "Hello, am I speaking with [Your Name]? I'm calling from Royal Hospital's Patient Data Quality team. We've noticed a discrepancy in your records, could you confirm your date of birth and NHS number so we can update our system?" ⚑ 1
YOU (Example Response) "Can I ask, why do you need my NHS number? Shouldn't you already have it?"
CALLER "It's a routine verification process following a system migration. I also need to confirm your home address and GP surgery. This is required to maintain your access to NHS services." ⚑ 2
⚑ Red Flag 1 - Unsolicited Call
The NHS does not call patients to request identity verification. If you didn't initiate the call, treat all requests for personal data with suspicion.
⚑ Red Flag 2 - Escalating Requests
The caller progressively asks for more sensitive data (DOB → NHS number → address → GP). Legitimate organisations ask for minimum data only.
⚑ Red Flag 3 - Threat of Service Loss
"Maintain your access to NHS services" is a pressure tactic. The NHS cannot suspend your access over a routine admin call.
✓ Your Rights
You have the right to end any call and call back on a verified number. Ask for a name, department, and reference number - a genuine caller will provide these without pressure.

If You Had Clicked
Your NHS login credentials and personal data would be harvested. Fraudsters use this to access your NHS records, commit identity theft, redirect prescriptions, or sell your data. Recovering from identity theft takes months and causes significant distress.
✅
The Safe Response
Verifying through official channels - nhs.uk, the NHS App, or by calling your GP - costs a few minutes but protects your health records, financial details, and personal safety. You can also report suspicious NHS messages to 7726 (text) or report@phishing.gov.uk.
Your Patient Safety Checklist
🔍
Check the Sender
Real NHS emails end in @nhs.net. Real NHS links go to nhs.uk. Any variation is a red flag.
⏸️
Pause on Urgency
If a message creates panic or a tight deadline, slow down. Urgency is the attacker's most powerful tool.
📲
Use the NHS App
Check appointments, prescriptions, and messages through the official NHS App - not links in texts or emails.
📣
Report It
Forward suspicious texts to 7726. Email phishing to report@phishing.gov.uk. Reporting protects other patients.
🔐
Protect Your NHS Login
Use a unique password for the NHS App and enable two-factor authentication. Never share your NHS login with anyone.
📞
Hang Up & Call Back
If a call feels suspicious, it's always safe to hang up and call the organisation back on a verified number from their website.
